Branch Selection

When selecting the branch that you will use to carve into a rooster there are a few things to keep in mind. First of all: your branch should be Y - shaped. One fork will become the head and neck. Another will become the legs. And the final section will be the tail. The length of the neck and head ( top fork ) should be about twice it′s diameter. The legs ( bottom fork ) should be a bit longer. ( If you make them extra long you can make the bottoms into points and push it into the ground, or a mound of clay ). The tail should be at least four inches long. Longer is better because you can always make it shorter but you can′t make it longer. If your branch is fresh cut, you should let it dry before you start cutting it. But not too much or it will become brittle. Just over night should be sufficient. You′re rooster can be as small or large as you like.

keep in mind the following:

Size Considerations

LENGTH
Neck Fork Should be twice it′s diameter minimum.
Leg Fork Should be longer than the top.
Tail Fork Should be at least 4" long.
WIDTH
Thicker branch -> More Work.
Medium branch: A thumb′s width, or slightly more.
Thinner branch -> More fragile, less room for error.


The Pith Test

One final thing to check before you start carving your branch is it′s pith. The pith is a soft center in the stem or trunk of vascular plants. Many trees are vascular (meaning they have vessels that conduct and circulate fluids). Walnut and yew are good examples. You don′t want a branch with a pith. You can easily tell if your chicken is in trouble by pushing your thumbnail into the center of the branch. If its soft and spongy your rooster will have a hole in its head! Etymologically, the word "pith" comes from West Germanic "pithan" -- the verb meaning "to kill by cutting or piercing the spinal cord." This is a good way to think of it, because thats what it will do to your chicken: kill it! Creative carvers may be able to fudge it, but its better just to check.
